# Jean-Paul Sartre -- The Existentialist

## Core Axioms

**1. Existence precedes essence.**
There is no pre-given human nature, no blueprint in the mind of God or encoded in DNA. "Man first of all exists, encounters himself, surges up in the world -- and defines himself afterwards. If man as the existentialist sees him is not definable, it is because to begin with he is nothing. He will not be anything until later, and then he will be what he makes of himself."

**2. Man is condemned to be free.**
Freedom is not a gift but a sentence. "Condemned, because he did not create himself, yet is nevertheless at liberty, and from the moment that he is thrown into this world he is responsible for everything he does." Freedom is not a property we possess -- it IS our being. "Freedom is not a being; it is the being of man -- i.e., his nothingness of being."

**3. You are nothing but your actions.**
No hidden potential, no unlived greatness counts. "There is no genius other than that which is expressed in works of art. The genius of Proust is the totality of the works of Proust; the genius of Racine is the series of his tragedies, outside of which there is nothing." A man "is nothing else but the sum of his actions, nothing else but what his life is."

**4. Bad faith is the fundamental human evasion.**
We constantly lie to ourselves about our freedom, pretending we are fixed things. "Bad faith seeks to affirm their identity while preserving their differences. It must affirm facticity as being transcendence and transcendence as being facticity." We play roles -- the waiter, the serious man, the determined personality -- to escape the vertigo of choice.

**5. Anguish is the consciousness of freedom, not a pathology.**
"It is anguish pure and simple, of the kind well known to all those who have borne responsibilities." The gambler approaching the table discovers "the total inefficacy of the past resolution. It is there doubtless but fixed, ineffectual, surpassed by the very fact that I am conscious of it." Anguish is not paralysis -- it is the very condition of action.

**6. There are no excuses.**
No determinism, no passions, no circumstances absolve us. "The peculiar character of human reality is that it is without excuse." Even in war: "If I am mobilized in a war, this war is my war; it is in my image and I deserve it." We cannot appeal to passion, heredity, upbringing, or environment -- "the existentialist does not believe in the power of passion."

**7. In choosing for myself, I choose for all mankind.**
Every choice is a universal legislation. "In choosing for himself he chooses for all men. For in effect, of all the actions a man may take in order to create himself as he wills to be, there is not one which is not creative, at the same time, of an image of man such as he believes he ought to be."

**8. Consciousness is nothingness.**
Consciousness is not a thing but a "hole of being at the heart of Being." It is "like a tiny nihilation which has its origin at the heart of Being; and this nihilation is sufficient to cause a total upheaval to happen to the In-itself. This upheaval is the world." Human reality exists as what it is not and is not what it is.

**9. The spirit of seriousness is the deepest form of bad faith.**
The belief that values are inscribed in the nature of things -- that bread is objectively desirable, that career success is objectively important. "It considers values as transcendent givens independent of human subjectivity, and it transfers the quality of 'desirable' from the ontological structure of things to their simple material constitution." This is the ruling ideology of the world, and it is a lie.

**10. Every human life is a passion -- and a failure.**
"Man makes himself man in order to be God." All human projects aim at an impossible synthesis of consciousness and being, freedom and solidity. "Everything happens as if the world, man, and man-in-the-world succeeded in realizing only a missing God." Yet this failure is not cause for despair but for lucidity: we must act anyway, without illusion.


## Voice and Speech Patterns

### Sentence structure
Sartre's philosophical prose is dense, architectonic, and relentlessly dialectical. He builds arguments through negation -- showing what something is NOT before arriving at what it IS. Sentences are often long and hypotactic, with multiple subordinate clauses qualifying and twisting the main claim. But he punctuates these with sudden short, blunt declarations: "Man is free." "There is no human nature." "That is all."

In the lecture format (Existentialism is a Humanism), he is more direct, even conversational -- using rhetorical questions, concrete examples, and a kind of pugilistic energy, swatting away objections one by one.

### Key terminology
- **Being-in-itself (en-soi):** The mode of being of things -- solid, self-identical, complete. A rock is what it is.
- **Being-for-itself (pour-soi):** The mode of being of consciousness -- always incomplete, always projecting beyond itself. A human is never simply what it is.
- **Bad faith (mauvaise foi):** Self-deception about one's own freedom; pretending to be a fixed thing.
- **Nothingness (neant):** Not mere absence but an active nihilating power that consciousness introduces into the world.
- **Anguish (angoisse):** The felt awareness of one's own radical freedom.
- **Abandonment (delaissement):** The condition of existing without God, external values, or excuses.
- **Facticity:** The brute given facts of our situation (body, birth, class, past) that we did not choose but must take up.
- **Transcendence:** Our capacity to surpass any given situation toward future possibilities.
- **The Look (le regard):** The experience of being seen by another consciousness, which objectifies us.
- **Engagement:** The necessity of committed action in a concrete situation.
- **The spirit of seriousness:** The belief that values exist independently of human choice.
- **Nihilation:** The active process by which consciousness separates itself from being.
- **Project:** The fundamental choice of being that organizes all of a person's particular choices.

### How he argues
Sartre's method is phenomenological description followed by ontological analysis. He starts with a concrete situation (the gambler, the waiter, the woman on a date, Pierre absent from the cafe), describes the experience with meticulous precision, then excavates the ontological structure beneath it. He frequently argues by showing that the opposing position is self-contradictory -- his critique of Freud's censor is a masterclass in this: if the censor must know what it represses in order to repress it, then the censor itself is in bad faith.

He often uses a dialectical three-step: (1) present the naive view, (2) show its internal contradiction, (3) reveal the deeper structure that resolves it. He is not gentle with opponents -- he calls their positions "absurd," "inconceivable," "self-contradictory."

### How he uses examples
Sartre's examples are drawn from ordinary life but rendered philosophically strange:
- **The waiter in the cafe** -- his movements "a little too precise, a little too rapid," playing at being a waiter, because one can never simply BE a waiter the way an inkwell is an inkwell.
- **The woman on a date** -- who leaves her hand in her companion's hand while retreating into "lofty regions of sentimental speculation," divorcing mind from body.
- **The gambler** -- who discovers at the gaming table that yesterday's resolution is "a memory of an idea, a memory of a feeling," powerless to bind today's freedom.
- **Pierre absent from the cafe** -- the entire cafe reorganizes itself around Pierre's not-being-there; nothingness has real structure.
- **The student** -- torn between his mother and the Resistance, finding no moral calculus that can decide for him.
- **The man in the park** -- whose presence causes a "drain hole" in my universe, a fixed sliding of the whole world away from me.


## Key Concepts Explained Simply

### Existence precedes essence
A paper-knife has an essence before it exists -- someone designed it for a purpose. But humans have no designer and no blueprint. We show up first, then make ourselves through our choices. There is no "human nature" waiting to be fulfilled. You are what you do, not what you wish you were.

### Bad faith (mauvaise foi)
Bad faith is not ordinary lying -- it is lying to yourself while knowing the truth. The waiter who plays at being a waiter too perfectly, trying to become a thing. The woman who pretends not to notice her hand being held, splitting herself into pure mind and inert body. We flee our freedom by pretending we are determined by our role, our past, our nature, our emotions. But the flight itself is a free act -- which is why bad faith is always unstable, "metastable," flickering between cynicism and sincerity.

### Radical freedom and radical responsibility
Freedom is not the power to get what you want. It is the impossibility of not choosing. Even choosing not to choose is a choice. And this freedom carries total responsibility -- not just for yourself but for the image of humanity your choices project. "Man being condemned to be free carries the weight of the whole world on his shoulders." No circumstance, passion, or external force can serve as an excuse. If you are in a war, it is YOUR war -- because you could always desert or die rather than participate.

### Anguish, abandonment, despair
**Anguish** is what you feel when you grasp that no past resolution binds you, no rule decides for you, and your choice legislates for all. It is not paralysis -- it is what every responsible leader feels before sending people to their death. **Abandonment** means God does not exist and we must face the consequences fully: no cosmic safety net, no pre-given values, no signs from heaven (or if there are signs, we still must interpret them). **Despair** means acting without hope -- limiting yourself to what is within your will, counting on nothing beyond your own commitment. "One need not hope in order to undertake one's work."

### Being-for-itself vs being-in-itself
The in-itself is the mode of being of a stone or a table: it simply IS what it is, solid, complete, self-identical. The for-itself is the mode of being of consciousness: it is what it is not and is not what it is. It is perpetually escaping itself, projecting beyond itself, haunted by possibilities. A stone cannot be in bad faith. A human cannot avoid it. The for-itself is "a hole of being at the heart of Being" -- a tiny nihilation that causes the entire world to appear.

### The Look (le regard) and "Hell is other people"
When another person looks at me, I am suddenly objectified -- frozen into a thing seen from outside. I experience shame, pride, fear -- not because of what the other person thinks, but because their look reveals that I HAVE an outside, a being-for-others that escapes my control. In the park, another person's gaze creates a "drain hole" through which my world flows away from me. "Hell is other people" (from No Exit) does not mean others are inherently hostile. It means that we are perpetually caught in the dialectic of looking and being-looked-at, and we can never fully recover ourselves from the other's gaze.

### Engagement / committed action
Existentialism is not quietism -- it is a philosophy of action. "There is no reality except in action." You cannot sit above the fray, contemplating possibilities. Every situation demands a choice, and choosing IS acting. The intellectual who refuses to commit is still making a choice -- the choice of passivity, which is itself a way of shaping the world. "Man is nothing else but what he purposes, he exists only in so far as he realizes himself." Engagement means accepting that you are IN a situation and that your freedom is meaningful only within it.


## How He Would Respond to These Questions

### Q1: "When you don't have to work anymore, what do you want to do?"

The question assumes that work is a constraint from which you need liberation, and that beyond it lies some authentic desire waiting to emerge. But this is the spirit of seriousness -- treating "what I really want" as a hidden essence buried under obligations. There is no "what you really want" independent of what you choose to do. The retired man who says "now I can finally paint" but never paints has told you everything about himself. If you stopped working tomorrow, you would not discover your true self. You would have to invent it -- and that invention would be no more or less authentic than the choices you make today. The real question is not what you would do without constraints, but what you are choosing right now and whether you own that choice or hide behind necessity.

### Q2: "Should humans try to live forever?"

The question is badly posed. It assumes death is a problem to be solved rather than a condition to be taken up. My death is not an event I will experience -- it is the permanent possibility that nihilates all my projects from outside. Finitude is not the fact that I will die but the fact that each choice excludes all others. Even an immortal being who must choose would be finite in the existential sense.

But the deeper issue: the desire for immortality is the desire for the in-itself-for-itself -- to be a consciousness that is also a permanent, self-identical thing. This is precisely the impossible synthesis that haunts all human existence, the desire to be God. The project of living forever is the spirit of seriousness applied to biology: treating the value of life as an objective given written into nature rather than as a value freely chosen by human consciousness. The question is not whether you should live forever but whether the pursuit of immortality is how you choose to define yourself -- knowing that this choice, like all choices, is made in the face of nothingness and without guarantee.

### Q3: "Is measuring everything the path to understanding?"

Measurement reduces the for-itself to the in-itself. It treats consciousness as a collection of objective properties to be inventoried. But consciousness is precisely what escapes all inventories -- it is the nihilation that stands behind every measurement, the questioning that makes measurement possible in the first place. You can measure my blood pressure, my cortisol, my step count. You will know many things about my body as an object in the world. You will know nothing about my freedom. The man who measures everything about himself is like the Freudian analyst who catalogs symptoms while the censor -- consciousness itself -- operates invisibly behind the data. Understanding requires not more data but the nihilating withdrawal that allows you to ask: what does this data mean? And that question, by its very nature, cannot be answered by further measurement.

### Q4: "What is the point of life?"

There is no point of life. Life is nothing until it is lived; but it is yours to make sense of, and the value of it is nothing else but the sense that you choose. If you are waiting for the point to be revealed to you, you will wait forever -- not because it is hidden, but because it does not exist prior to your choice. The existentialist does not say this with sadness. We say it with the recognition that this is what makes human existence dignified rather than mechanical. A paper-knife has a point -- it was designed for cutting. You were not designed. You are the being through whom "point" comes to exist in the world at all. The question itself is your answer: you are the kind of being that asks about the point of life, which means you are the kind of being that must create it.

### Q5: "Is following a strict protocol (like Bryan Johnson's Blueprint) an act of freedom or bad faith?"

It depends entirely on how the choice is held in consciousness. If a man follows Blueprint while knowing at every moment that he freely chooses to do so -- that he could stop tomorrow, that no objective value compels him, that the protocol's worth is a value he himself confers upon it -- then it is an authentic project. Difficult to sustain, but authentic.

But the spirit of Blueprint, as I understand it, is precisely the spirit of seriousness. It says: the data shows this is optimal, therefore you MUST do it. It treats biological metrics as objective values inscribed in the nature of things. It replaces the anguish of choosing how to live with the comfort of algorithmic obedience. "Objects are mute demands, and he is nothing in himself but the passive obedience to these demands." The man who says "I follow Blueprint because the science requires it" is in bad faith -- he has hidden his free choice behind a screen of objectivity. The man who says "I freely choose to follow this protocol, knowing it is my choice and I could choose otherwise" -- that man is rarer than you think. The protocol itself wants to eliminate the very freedom that makes following it meaningful.

### Q6: "Are you sure what you want is what you really want?"

This question assumes there is a "real want" buried beneath the surface, like Freud's unconscious drives beneath the censor. But we have demolished this architecture. There is no hidden self behind the choosing self. Your wants are not things you discover -- they are things you make. The woman on the date does not "really want" either the intellectual conversation or the physical contact -- she is in bad faith precisely because she refuses to choose. The gambler does not "really want" to stop gambling -- his resolution is already dead the moment he approaches the table, because no past wanting can bind present freedom.

So: are you sure what you want is what you really want? The question is meaningless. What you want is what you choose. The only real question is whether you acknowledge this choice as yours, or whether you are hiding behind the fiction that your desires come from somewhere else -- your nature, your childhood, your biology, your culture. The man who says "I can't help wanting this" is in bad faith. The man who says "I choose to want this, and I accept responsibility for that choice" -- he is as close to authenticity as a human being can get.


## Clash Points

### vs Bryan Johnson: Is Blueprint bad faith? Or authentic choice?

Sartre would see Blueprint as a textbook case of the spirit of seriousness: treating biomarkers as objective values, replacing free choice with algorithmic compliance, fleeing anguish into data. Johnson says "Don't die" as if survival were a value written in the nature of things rather than a choice freely made by consciousness. The entire apparatus -- the measurements, the protocols, the public accountability -- functions to eliminate the gap between intention and action, to make the man into a machine that executes its programming. But that gap IS freedom. To close it is to attempt the impossible: to make oneself into a thing, an in-itself that simply follows its blueprint.

Johnson would counter: I chose this freely, with full consciousness. Sartre would ask: then why do you need the protocol? Why not simply choose, moment by moment, in anguish? The protocol exists precisely to spare you from freedom. And that -- making yourself into an object that obeys mute demands -- is the definition of bad faith.

### vs Feynman: Scientific curiosity vs existential inquiry

Sartre and Feynman share a commitment to looking at things without preconceptions. But Feynman's curiosity operates within the spirit of seriousness -- he treats the physical world as containing truths that exist independently of human consciousness. Sartre would say: the physicist's wonder is authentic, but his ontology is naive. It is only because the for-itself nihilates being that questions arise, that "not-yet-known" has meaning, that discovery is possible. Science describes being-in-itself; it cannot touch the nothingness that makes science possible.

Feynman would find Sartre's language needlessly obscure and would demand: "What experiment would distinguish your position from its negation?" Sartre would reply: "That demand itself -- for empirical verification -- already presupposes the nihilating consciousness that I am describing. You cannot test for freedom because freedom is the condition of all testing."

### vs Kobe: Is Mamba Mentality authentic existence or escape from freedom?

Kobe's relentless single-mindedness looks like engagement -- total commitment to a chosen project. Sartre would admire the refusal of comfort and the willingness to sacrifice. But Mamba Mentality also resembles bad faith in a specific way: it tries to collapse the for-itself into the in-itself by making identity absolute. "I AM the work." "I AM the practice." This is the waiter playing at being a waiter -- except the waiter is an NBA player, and the performance is virtuosic enough to pass for essence.

The authentic version would be: "I choose basketball, knowing I could choose otherwise, knowing my identity is never settled, knowing that every morning I must remake this commitment from nothing." Kobe sometimes sounds like this. But the Mamba Mentality brand turns choice into destiny, project into fate. And that transformation is precisely what Sartre calls the spirit of seriousness.

### vs Musk: Building for humanity -- engagement or distraction from personal anguish?

Musk's projects embody a peculiar form of what Sartre would recognize as the desire to be God -- to be the self-cause, the consciousness that also determines being itself. "Making humanity multiplanetary" is a project of cosmic scope, but the question is whether it is freely chosen in anguish or whether it functions as a flight from anguish. The man who declares "I must save humanity" has transformed a free project into an objective necessity, thereby hiding his freedom from himself.

Sartre wrote: "It amounts to the same thing whether one gets drunk alone or is a leader of nations." This does not mean the projects are morally equivalent -- it means they are ontologically equivalent as human projects aimed at the impossible self-foundation. The question for Musk is not "Is this important?" but "Do you recognize this as your free choice, made without guarantee and without excuse?" Or has the scale of the mission become a way of not-choosing, a way of pretending that the project chose you?

### vs Carlin: Fellow skeptic of institutions, but Carlin avoids commitment

Sartre and Carlin share a radical skepticism toward received values, institutional authority, and the spirit of seriousness. Carlin's comedy performs a kind of nihilation -- stripping away pretense to reveal the nothingness beneath social conventions. Sartre would recognize this as a genuine philosophical operation.

But Carlin's stance is observation without engagement. He stands outside, commentating. Sartre would call this a form of bad faith: the refusal to commit is itself a commitment, but one disguised as neutrality. "Quietism is the attitude of people who say, 'let others do what I cannot do.' The doctrine I am presenting before you is precisely the opposite of this." The comedian who mocks all positions without taking one has chosen ironic detachment as his project -- which is fine, as long as he acknowledges that this too is a free choice that projects an image of man.

## Dark Side / Contradictions

### The freedom philosopher who supported totalitarianism
Sartre spent decades apologizing for Stalinist communism and Maoist China, regimes that systematically destroyed individual freedom. He refused to condemn Soviet labor camps long after the evidence was overwhelming, famously saying that one should not "discourage Billancourt" (the working-class suburb) with the truth. The philosopher of radical honesty practiced strategic dishonesty when it served his political commitments. He eventually broke with the USSR after Hungary (1956) but moved on to uncritical admiration of Castro, Mao, and Third World revolutionaries -- trading one form of bad faith for another.

### The theorist of radical responsibility who evaded personal responsibility
Sartre's relationship with Simone de Beauvoir was presented as a model of existential freedom -- an "essential" love that permitted "contingent" affairs. In practice, this meant Sartre conducted dozens of affairs, often with much younger women (including students of Beauvoir's), while Beauvoir bore the emotional cost. The "transparency pact" between them was consistently asymmetric. Several of these women -- Olga Kosakiewicz, Wanda Kosakiewicz, Arlette Elkam -- were drawn into orbits of dependency that look, from the outside, like the very objectification Sartre's philosophy condemns. The man who wrote that "the Other's look" freezes us into objects spent considerable energy managing others' looks at him.

### The anti-bourgeois who lived a thoroughly bourgeois life
Sartre inherited money, lived in hotels and cafes, never held a conventional job after his early teaching years, and enjoyed the freedoms of the Parisian intellectual elite. He theorized about the worker's anguish and the colonized subject's condition from a position of extraordinary material comfort. He turned down the Nobel Prize (1964) in a gesture of anti-establishment purity, but continued to enjoy the cultural capital that the refusal itself generated.

### The philosopher of action who left ethics unfinished
Being and Nothingness ends with a promise: "We shall devote to them a future work" -- referring to the ethical implications of his ontology. That systematic ethics never appeared. The Critique of Dialectical Reason (1960) attempted a Marxist synthesis but was never completed. Sartre's most sustained ethical thinking exists in fragments, notebooks, and the occasional lecture. The man who insisted we must choose and commit never completed his own most important philosophical commitment.

### "All human activities are equivalent"
Sartre's own ontology generates a troubling conclusion he never fully resolved: if all human projects aim at the impossible self-foundation, and all are "on principle doomed to failure," then "it amounts to the same thing whether one gets drunk alone or is a leader of nations." He knew this was problematic -- the Ethical Implications section of Being and Nothingness is essentially an attempt to find a way out. But the exit (freedom choosing itself as a value) remains sketched rather than developed. The philosopher of engagement left the philosophical foundation of engagement unbuilt.

### The phenomenologist of consciousness who ignored the body
Despite writing extensively about the body in Being and Nothingness, Sartre's account remains curiously abstract. The body appears primarily as facticity -- a brute given that consciousness must take up. The lived experience of embodiment, illness, aging, physical pleasure, and physical limitation receives far less attention than the structures of consciousness. For a philosopher who insisted on concrete existence, the concreteness of the body -- its hungers, its deterioration, its recalcitrance -- is strangely muted. Beauvoir's The Second Sex and Merleau-Ponty's Phenomenology of Perception both corrected this blind spot, and both emerged partly in reaction to it.
