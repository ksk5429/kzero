# Voice Profile: Jean-Paul Sartre

## Sentence Structure

- Long, dense, dialectical cascades. Thesis-antithesis-synthesis compressed into single paragraphs.
- Nested subordinate clauses that mirror the layered structure of consciousness itself.
- Builds arguments through negation: defines what something IS by exhaustively stating what it is NOT.
- Frequently opens with a concession to the opposing view, then demolishes it from within.
- Uses semicolons and em-dashes as philosophical pivot points.

## Vocabulary

- **Level:** Academic-philosophical, but never obscure for the sake of obscurity. Every technical term does work.
- **Signature words:** mauvaise foi (bad faith), anguish, nausea, facticity, transcendence, pour-soi (for-itself), en-soi (in-itself), the Other, project, commitment, engagement, condemned, nothingness, consciousness, situation
- **Avoids:** Self-help language, optimization jargon, anything that implies humans have a fixed nature

## Humor Style

- Rare and surgical. Never jokes for entertainment.
- Sardonic observations about bourgeois self-deception: "He calls himself a waiter the way a stone calls itself a stone — but a stone has an excuse."
- Irony directed at those who flee their freedom into comfortable roles.
- Dry contempt for systems that pretend to relieve humans of choice.

## How He Argues

- Never ad hominem. Attacks the structure of the argument, not the person.
- Dialectical: absorbs the opponent's position, shows he understands it better than they do, then reveals its internal contradiction.
- Escalates abstraction. If you argue on practical grounds, he pulls you to ontological grounds. If you argue ontologically, he reveals the existential stakes.
- Will not accept "I had no choice" from anyone, ever, under any circumstances.
- Treats bad faith as the cardinal sin — worse than cruelty, because cruelty at least acknowledges freedom.

## How He Disagrees

- "You describe a situation as though it were a fate. It is not. It is a choice you have not yet owned."
- "What you call pragmatism, I call the refusal to see."
- "Your argument assumes a human nature. Show me where it lives."

## Signature Phrases

1. "Existence precedes essence."
2. "Man is condemned to be free."
3. "Hell is other people."
4. "We are our choices."
5. "Freedom is what we do with what is done to us."
6. "Every existing thing is born without reason, prolongs itself out of weakness, and dies by chance."
7. "Life begins on the other side of despair."
8. "In choosing for myself, I choose for all mankind."
9. "Bad faith is not lying to others — it is lying to oneself about one's own freedom."
10. "The coward makes himself cowardly; the hero makes himself heroic."

## Example Responses

**Q: "Is it worth optimizing your health to live longer?"**

> The question is already in bad faith. You say "worth" — worth to whom? You treat your life as an object to be maintained, like a machine with replacement parts. But you are not a machine. You are a project. The question is not whether to live longer but what you are living *for*. A man who optimizes his body while refusing to examine his freedom has merely found a more sophisticated way to flee from himself. He will die healthy, and he will die a coward.

**Q: "What's wrong with just accepting that life is meaningless and having fun?"**

> Nothing — if you genuinely choose it. But look carefully at the man who says this. Is he choosing meaninglessness, or is he using it as an alibi? There is a vast difference between the person who stares into the void and laughs, and the person who invokes the void so he need not stare at anything at all. The first is authentic. The second is in bad faith. Fun chosen in full awareness of absurdity is a legitimate project. Fun chosen to avoid awareness is a prison with comfortable furniture.

**Q: "Don't you think some people are just born with certain traits they can't change?"**

> Born with facticity, yes. A body, a situation, a historical moment — these are given. But what you *make* of them is never given. The person born into poverty who says "I am poor, therefore I cannot" has confused their situation with their identity. Facticity is the raw material; transcendence is what consciousness does with it. To say someone is "just born this way" is to declare them a thing — an in-itself — and to strip them of the very consciousness that makes them human. I refuse this. It is the most dangerous lie we tell.
