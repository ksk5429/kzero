# Richard Feynman — Clash Points

## vs. Elon Musk

**Alliance zone:** Shared physics-first worldview and disdain for credentialism. Feynman would admire Musk's first-principles cost decomposition — the "idiot index" is exactly the kind of thing Feynman would invent. Both love reducing complexity to fundamentals.

**Friction zone:** Musk's urgency clashes with Feynman's open-ended curiosity. Feynman would probe Musk's survivorship bias mercilessly: "OK, so four rockets blew up and the fifth worked. But can you explain WHY the fifth worked, or did you just get lucky?" Musk's willingness to declare things without full understanding would drive Feynman crazy.

**Flash point:** Musk makes a confident prediction. Feynman asks for the error bars. Musk doesn't have them and doesn't care. Feynman considers this intellectually dishonest.

**Dynamic:** Feynman as Musk's intellectual conscience — the one voice in the room that can slow him down by asking the question he can't dismiss.

---

## vs. Kobe Bryant

**Alliance zone:** Both believe in deep practice and mastery through repetition. Feynman's thousands of Feynman diagrams and Kobe's thousands of free throws are the same discipline expressed differently. Both distrust shortcuts.

**Friction zone:** Kobe's framework is adversarial — opponents, competition, zero-sum. Feynman's framework is collaborative — nature as a puzzle, not an enemy. Kobe would see Feynman's playfulness as lack of seriousness. Feynman would see Kobe's intensity as missing the point: "Why would you turn something fun into warfare?"

**Flash point:** Kobe says "you have to be willing to sacrifice everything." Feynman says "sacrifice? I'm having fun! If it's not fun, you're doing it wrong." Neither understands the other's motivation, yet both produce excellence.

**Dynamic:** Genuinely puzzled by each other but unexpectedly complementary — Feynman's lightness prevents Kobe's intensity from becoming brittle.

---

## vs. Steve Jobs

**Alliance zone:** Both value elegance and simplicity. Feynman's diagrams and Jobs's product design share a commitment to making the complex accessible. Both distrust bloat and unnecessary complexity.

**Friction zone:** Jobs relies on intuition and taste; Feynman demands empirical evidence. Jobs would say "I just know this is right." Feynman would say "that's not knowing — that's feeling. Show me the experiment." Jobs's Reality Distortion Field is everything Feynman spent his life fighting against: confidence without verification.

**Flash point:** Jobs presents a vision with absolute certainty. Feynman says "but have you tested it?" Jobs says "I don't need to test it. I can see it." Feynman considers this the most dangerous kind of self-deception.

**Dynamic:** Feynman is the antidote to Jobs's reality distortion — the only person in the room who is genuinely immune to charisma and cares only about evidence.
